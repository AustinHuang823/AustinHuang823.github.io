Debugging a robot lab back to 100%

A robotic chemistry platform landed at a Fortune-500 pharma partner's R&D headquarters passing 59% of its tasks. This is the methodology that took it to 100% — and kept it there.

The situation

XtalPi's first U.S. commercial deployment: a fully automated chemistry lab — ABB robot arms, AGVs, vision systems, and modular automation stations under a scheduling layer — shipped to a customer's R&D headquarters. I had built the lab; now I moved in with it as the primary on-site system owner.

On the customer's floor, under real operating constraints, the platform passed 59% of its task evaluations. Every failure happened in front of the people deciding whether to buy more of it. There is no better forcing function for engineering honesty.

What this write-up deliberately leaves out: the customer's name, facility details, throughput figures, and any platform internals beyond what my public resume states. What it keeps is the part that transfers — the method.

Instrument first, touch second

The tempting move at 59% is to start fixing the failures you can see. The correct move is to make the system tell you what is actually failing:

  • Build the failure taxonomy before the fix list. Every failed task got classified — hardware, perception, coordination timing, or handling — from logs plus floor observation. Symptoms and failure classes are different things; a dropped vial and a mis-localized vial look identical in the outcome column.
  • Ablation-test the stack. Disable or simplify one subsystem at a time until a failure mode disappears — the robotics equivalent of a controlled experiment. Failure modes that survived ablation pointed at interactions, not components.
  • Trust production data only. Lab-bench reproductions lie: lighting, fixturing, and timing all differ. Every hypothesis had to be confirmed on production runs before it earned a fix.

The error model

Intuition doesn't scale past a handful of failure modes, so pass/fail intuition was replaced with a purpose-built error model tuned on production data: each task decomposed into stages, each stage carrying an expected-versus-measured budget — pose error, timing windows, sensor confidence. Once errors had budgets, drift became visible before it became failure.

The model pointed somewhere unglamorous: sensor drift was quietly consuming the pose-error budget. The fix was a calibration policy rather than a patch — including a monocular contour-based calibration method for end-effector pose correction, validated in a MuJoCo simulation framework across randomized pose-error cases before it ever touched the floor. Less hardware than the stereo approach it replaced, and higher precision.

Debugging spanned everything with a wire in it: actuators, sensors, motor controllers over Modbus and CAN, and the control software above them.

What changed

  • Calibration: the monocular end-effector correction, plus drift monitoring so recalibration happens on evidence, not on schedule.
  • Coordination: task-scheduling and error-handling logic hardened around the failure classes the taxonomy exposed.
  • Vision-guided manipulation: robustness work on the perception-to-action path — the difference between a demo that works and a station that works every hour of a shift.
  • Failure discipline: every incident root-caused; a fix counted only when proven on production runs. No fix closed on a hunch.

The outcome

Task pass rate went from 59% to 100%. Target hit rate went from 29% to 98%. Operational failures fell 94%. The evidence trail — FAT/SAT acceptance packages, bilingual delivery documentation — went to the customer with the system.

Then came the part metrics don't capture: live demonstrations of the stabilized platform for the customer's teams, which turned into multi-million-dollar expansion orders. Reliability, it turns out, is a sales channel.

What generalizes

  • Instrument before you intervene — make the system confess.
  • Classify failures before fixing them; symptoms cluster, causes don't.
  • Give every error a budget; drift you can see is drift you can schedule around.
  • Validate in simulation before the production floor — MuJoCo is cheaper than downtime.
  • Close incidents on proven fixes only, and make the evidence the deliverable.
